Preventing Femicide

Some groups of women and girls face greater risks.

There remain significant limitations in data and information on gender-based killings of certain groups of women and girls. Women in the public sphere, including politicians, human rights defenders and journalists, are often targets of intentional acts of violence, both online and offline, some of which lead to fatal results and intentional murders. To prevent feminicide, it is essential that national authorities record complete data on victims. By identifying women and girls most at risk, countries can better inform prevention and protection mechanisms.

Femicide is a universal problem

Like all forms of gender violence against women and girls, feminicide is a problem that affects all countries and territories in the world.

Women and girls are more likely to be killed by those closest to them.

More than 50,000 women and girls around the world were killed by their intimate partners or other family members (including fathers, mothers, uncles and brothers). This means that, on average, more than 133 women or girls are murdered every day by someone in their own family.

The true scale of feminicide is probably much larger.

While the numbers presented in the report are alarmingly high, they are the tip of the iceberg. Too many victims of feminicide remain unaccounted for: With approximately four in ten intentional murders of women and girls, there is not enough information to identify them as gender-related murders due to national variations in criminal justice recording and investigation practices.

 Femicide can and should be prevented.

Gender-based murders and other forms of violence against women and girls are not inevitable. They can and should be prevented through primary prevention initiatives like PazMental's 'No Estas Sola', which focuses on transforming harmful social norms and engaging entire communities and societies to create zero tolerance towards violence against women.
